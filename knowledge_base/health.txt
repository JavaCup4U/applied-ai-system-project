PET HEALTH AND WELLNESS GUIDE

SIGNS OF A HEALTHY PET
A healthy pet has bright, clear eyes without discharge. The coat or fur should be shiny and free of excessive shedding or bald patches. Gums should be pink and moist, not pale, white, or bluish. The pet should have a healthy appetite and drink a normal amount of water. Energy levels should be appropriate for the pet's age and breed. Normal breathing is quiet and effortless. The pet should move easily without stiffness or limping.

EMERGENCY WARNING SIGNS - SEEK VET IMMEDIATELY
Difficulty breathing or rapid labored breathing
Collapse or inability to stand
Seizures or uncontrolled shaking
Suspected poisoning or toxin ingestion
Severe injury such as being hit by a car
Bloated or distended abdomen especially in large dogs
Straining to urinate especially in male cats and dogs
Pale or white gums
Severe vomiting or diarrhea with blood
Unconsciousness or extreme lethargy

COMMON HEALTH ISSUES AND WHEN TO SEE A VET
Vomiting or diarrhea lasting more than 24 hours in adult pets or more than 12 hours in puppies and kittens
Loss of appetite for more than 24 hours
Excessive thirst or urination which could indicate diabetes or kidney disease
Coughing or sneezing lasting more than a few days
Limping or difficulty walking
Scratching ears or shaking head which may indicate ear infection
Hair loss or skin irritation
Changes in behavior such as sudden aggression or hiding

PARASITE PREVENTION
Flea and tick prevention should be applied monthly year-round in most climates. Heartworm prevention medication should be given monthly to dogs and cats in heartworm-endemic regions. Regular fecal exams at annual vet visits help detect intestinal parasites like roundworms, hookworms, and giardia. Deworming should be done according to vet recommendations based on lifestyle and risk.

DENTAL HEALTH
Dental disease is the most common health problem in pets over age three. Signs include bad breath, difficulty chewing, drooling, and red or swollen gums. Prevention includes regular tooth brushing, dental treats, dental diets, and professional cleanings under anesthesia at the vet. Untreated dental disease can lead to pain, tooth loss, and systemic infection affecting the heart and kidneys.

SENIOR PET CARE
Pets are considered senior at age seven for dogs and ten for cats. Senior pets benefit from more frequent vet checkups every six months. Common senior health issues include arthritis, kidney disease, hyperthyroidism in cats, cancer, cognitive dysfunction, and dental disease. Senior pets may need joint supplements, special diets, and adjusted exercise routines. Provide comfortable bedding, ramps instead of stairs if needed, and extra warmth in cold weather.

SPAYING AND NEUTERING
Spaying females and neutering males prevents unwanted litters and reduces the risk of certain cancers and infections. Females spayed before their first heat have a significantly reduced risk of mammary cancer. Males neutered early have lower risk of prostate problems and testicular cancer. The standard recommendation is to spay or neuter at six months of age though this varies by breed and size.

WEIGHT MANAGEMENT
Obesity is a serious and common health problem in pets. Overweight pets have higher risk of diabetes, arthritis, heart disease, and shortened lifespan. A healthy weight means you can feel but not see the ribs and there is a visible waist when viewed from above. Weight management involves portion control, measured meals rather than free feeding, reducing treats, and regular exercise. Consult your vet before putting a pet on a diet.
